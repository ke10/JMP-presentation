Motivation

Agenda-setter makes a policy proposal that must be approved by voters.

The proposal can be revised when it gets rejected.

  • The president nominates federal judges, approved by senators
  • Chairs make motions, approved by committee members
  • Municipal authorities propose spending measures, approved by eligible voters

Key features

Voters are privately informed about their ideal policies.

Voters have conflicting preferences.

Questions

Does the agenda-setter value the ability to revise proposals?

How the adopted policies depend on the preferences of voters?

What is the role of voting rule?

Environment

  • Two voters, $L$ and $R$, and the agenda-setter $A$
  • The policy space is $\mathbb R_+$, the status-quo policy is $0$
  • The agenda-setter's period utility from policy $x$ is $u_A(x)=x$
  • The utilities of voters depend on state $\omega\in\{\ell,h\}$

Quadratic utilities

Period utility of voter $i$ from policy $x$ in state $\omega$ is \[u_i(x;\omega)=-\textstyle\left(\frac12 y_i^{\omega}-x\right)^2\]

Assumptions

Denote the (doubled) ideal policies of voter $L$ using $y^\ell>0$ and $y^h>0$.

Assume that the (doubled) ideal policies of voter $R$ are $y^\ell+b$ and $y^h+b$ for $b\geq0$.

Assume $y^\ell < y^h$.

Information

The agenda-setter is uninformed

Voters privately observe the state $\omega\in\{\ell,h\}$


The prior belief is $\hat\mu$ — the probability that the state is $h$.

Equilibrium concept

Perfect Bayesian equilibrium: assessment $(\sigma,\mathcal M)$ such that

  • strategies in $\sigma$ are sequentially rational
  • beliefs in $\mathcal M$ are consistent

Second period

Voter $i$ in state $\omega$ accepts $p_2$ iff $p_2\leq y^\omega_i$

The agenda-setter proposes $y^h_d$ if $\hat\mu>\frac{y^\ell_d}{y^h_d}$ and $y^\ell_d$ otherwise

Majority

The agenda-setter does not need to reach out to voter $L$.

Off-path beliefs do not matter.

Unique equilibrium path.

Majority

Theorem 1. Under the majority rule, there is a unique equilibrum path. There exist $m^\ell\in(0,1)$ and $m^h\in(0,1)$ such that:
  • if $\hat\mu < m^\ell$, the initial proposal is $y^\ell+b$
  • if $\hat\mu > m^h$, the initial proposal is $y^h+b$ and the revised proposal is $y^h+b$
  • if $m^\ell < \hat\mu < m^h$, the initial proposal is $p_R\in(y^\ell+b,y^h+b)$ and the revised proposal is $y^\ell+b$

Majority

Unanimity

Revised proposal depends on who voted for the initial proposal.

Can use off-path beliefs to punish deviations.

Multiplicity of equilibrium paths.

Theorem 2. There exist $\underline{v}$ and $\overline{v}$ such that under the unanimity rule, the agenda-setter's equilibrium ex ante expected payoff equals $v$ if and only if $v\in[\underline{v},\overline{v}]$.

Comparison

Trade-off between the ability to use punishments and having to reach out to voter $L$.

"Theorem 3." Either force can dominate, depending on the prior belief $\hat\mu$, bias $b$, and the ideal points $y^\ell$ and $y^h$.

Communication

The approach to communication is model-free.

Coalition-proofness – a refinement suggested by
Bernheim, Peleg, and Whinston (1987).

Equilibrium must be robust to joint deviations that also
self-enforcing.

Theorem 4. Restricting attention to coalition-proof equilibria, the majority rule always dominates the unanimity rule.
